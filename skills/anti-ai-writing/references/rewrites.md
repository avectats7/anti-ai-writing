# Rewrites: How to Fix Each Kind of Violation

A rewrite is not a substitution. A rewrite preserves meaning and improves the line. Apply these patterns instead of doing blind find-and-replace.

## Word-level rewrites

The principle: replace with the natural word a human writer would have used. Often the natural word is shorter.

| Banned | Use instead |
|---|---|
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help, make, enable (if "enable" reads naturally, otherwise cut) |
| leverage (verb) | use, draw on, build on, exploit (depending on context) |
| robust | strong, reliable, sturdy, well-built, or cut if filler |
| harness | use, channel, put to work, or cut if filler |
| unlock | reach, get to, find, open, or cut if filler |
| unleash | release, set loose, or cut if filler |
| showcase | show, present, display |
| streamline | simplify, speed up, cut steps from |
| seamless | smooth, easy, without friction, or cut if filler |
| synergy | (almost always cut entirely; rewrite the sentence) |
| innovative | new, fresh, original, or cut |
| comprehensive | complete, full, thorough |
| cutting-edge | new, current, modern, or cut |
| vibrant | lively, busy, bright, or cut |
| navigate (figurative) | get through, work with, handle, deal with |
| transformative | changes, reshapes (verb) |
| revolutionary | new, first (if true), or cut |
| game-changer | changes things, matters, or cut |
| groundbreaking | first, new, or cut |
| pivotal | key (no, key is also banned). Important. Decisive. |
| crucial | important, decisive, the centre of |
| delve | look at, dig into, study, examine |
| explore | look at, consider, study (or cut if filler) |
| empower | give the tools to, let, equip |
| foster | build, support, create the conditions for |
| spearhead | lead, run, drive (if "drive" is concrete) |
| valuable | useful, worth something specific (name the value) |
| noteworthy | worth noting (but usually cut: just state the fact) |

A few rules of thumb:

1. If a banned word can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.
2. If a banned word can be replaced by a shorter Saxon-root word, replace it.
3. If the only natural replacement is another banned word ("robust" → "strong" is fine, but "robust" → "comprehensive" is not), keep cutting.

## Phrase-level rewrites

Most banned phrases should be cut, not replaced. The phrase was filler.

| Banned phrase | What to do |
|---|---|
| "In today's world" / "In today's fast-paced world" / "In the age of" | Cut. Start the sentence at the first content word. |
| "It is worth noting that" / "It's important to note that" | Cut. The thing you were about to say is the actual sentence. |
| "Needless to say" / "As you may already know" | Cut. If it really is needless, do not say it. If the reader does not know, do not insult them. |
| "Now more than ever" | Cut. |
| "First and foremost" | Cut. Or use "First." |
| "Unlock the potential of X" | "Get more from X" / "Use X properly" / depending on context. |
| "Harness the power of X" | "Use X" |
| "Pave the way for X" | "Lead to X" / "Make X possible" |
| "Drive meaningful impact" | Cut. Replace with the specific impact you mean. |
| "Move the needle" | Cut. Replace with the specific metric you mean. |
| "Bridging the gap between X and Y" | "Connecting X and Y" or rewrite around what the connection actually does. |
| "In conclusion" / "In summary" / "To sum up" | Cut. If the piece does need a summary line, write one. Do not announce that you are about to summarise. |
| "At the end of the day" | Cut. The end of the day is a banned phrase wearing a hat. |
| "It's not X, it's Y" | Rewrite as two separate sentences. "It is Y." Then explain what makes it Y rather than X. |
| "Not just X, but Y" | Often cut. If the contrast matters, write two clean sentences. |

The general fix: the banned phrase usually has a one-word or zero-word replacement. The piece is shorter and sharper after the cut.

## Em dash rewrites

Em dashes (—) and en dashes (–) appear in three common positions. Each has a fix.

### Position 1: parenthetical aside
Original: "The report — which arrived three days late — was wrong."
Fix A (parentheses): "The report (which arrived three days late) was wrong."
Fix B (split sentence): "The report was wrong. It arrived three days late."
Fix C (commas): "The report, which arrived three days late, was wrong."

### Position 2: extension or example after a clause
Original: "We tried three approaches — direct mail, paid social, and outdoor."
Fix A (colon): "We tried three approaches: direct mail, paid social, outdoor."
Fix B (separate sentence): "We tried three approaches. Direct mail. Paid social. Outdoor."

### Position 3: emphasis after a strong claim
Original: "He was the best copywriter of his generation — and he never won an award."
Fix A (period): "He was the best copywriter of his generation. He never won an award."
Fix B (colon): "He was the best copywriter of his generation: he never won an award."
Fix C (rewrite): "The best copywriter of his generation never won an award."

### Compound-word hyphens

"Go-to-market" → "GTM" (if the reader knows the acronym) or "go to market" (if not).
"Pre-launch" → "before launch."
"Mid-raise" → "mid raise" or reword.
"In-depth" → "thorough" or "detailed."
"State-of-the-art" → "the best available" or rewrite.

If the term is a proper name (e.g. "X-Files," "Coca-Cola"), keep the hyphen. The rule is about punctuation, not about names.

## Rule of three rewrites

Diagnose first. Sometimes the three items are doing real work. The fix is needed only when the three are interchangeable filler.

Original: "Our platform is fast, simple, and powerful."
Fix A (two items, sharper): "Our platform is simple and fast."
Fix B (four items with specifics): "Our platform is simple to set up, fast to run, easy to leave, and priced by the second."
Fix C (drop the list, name the proof): "Our platform sets up in 90 seconds and runs in real time."

The key question: did each of the three items add something? If yes, you might keep all three (but check whether four is more honest). If the items were interchangeable, the list was decoration.

## Bulleted bold title rewrites

Original:
- **Speed:** Our service processes orders in 30 seconds.
- **Reliability:** 99.97% uptime, every quarter.
- **Cost:** Pricing starts at $0 for the first 10,000 requests.

Diagnosis: the bolded title is usually a one-word restatement of what follows. The list looks like a feature grid; it reads like an LLM filled out a template.

Fix A (prose):
> Orders process in 30 seconds. Uptime stayed at 99.97% last quarter. Pricing starts at zero for the first 10,000 requests.

Fix B (plain bullets, no bold titles):
- Orders process in 30 seconds.
- Uptime is 99.97% (last quarter).
- Pricing starts at zero for the first 10,000 requests.

Fix C (table, when the comparison really is tabular):
| Metric | Current |
|---|---|
| Order time | 30 seconds |
| Uptime (last Q) | 99.97% |
| Starting price | $0 (first 10k requests) |

Pick the form that fits the content. Prose is the default. Lists when truly list-shaped. Tables when truly comparison.

## Formulaic transition rewrites

| Banned | Fix |
|---|---|
| Furthermore, | cut. Start the new sentence with its content. |
| Moreover, | cut. |
| Additionally, | cut. |
| Subsequently, | cut, or replace with "after that" if the temporal beat matters. |
| Accordingly, | cut, or replace with "so" if the consequence matters. |
| In addition, | cut. |
| As previously mentioned, | cut. The reader remembers. If they did not, the original mention was weak. |
| As noted above, | cut. |
| With that in mind, | cut. |

The general fix: cut the transition word. Read the two paragraphs together. If they do not connect, the connection was weak; either tighten the second paragraph or merge them.

## Weak verb rewrites

| Weak | Direct |
|---|---|
| "X serves as a bridge between A and B." | "X bridges A and B." |
| "Y acts as a catalyst for Z." | "Y triggers Z." |
| "This feature serves as a way to..." | Cut the whole construction. "This feature [verb] X." |
| "The product features advanced..." | "The product has..." |
| "We facilitate the process of..." | "We [verb] X." Or cut the verb entirely. |
| "This tool is designed to help you..." | "This tool helps you..." |
| "The platform is positioned to..." | "The platform will..." or cut "is positioned to." |

The principle: every "is designed to," "serves as," "acts as," "is positioned to" is a buffer between subject and verb. Remove the buffer. The sentence is half as long and twice as strong.

## Sentence-level rewrites

Sometimes a sentence has so many violations that the right move is to rewrite it from scratch, not patch it.

Trigger conditions for a from-scratch rewrite:
- Three or more banned words in one sentence.
- A banned phrase plus a banned structure together.
- The sentence reads like a list of buzzwords with verbs between them.

Method:
1. Read the sentence and ask: what is the writer actually trying to say?
2. Write the simplest version of that idea.
3. Add back specificity (a number, a name, a concrete detail).
4. Read aloud.

Example:
Original: "In today's fast-paced world, our innovative platform empowers users to seamlessly navigate complex workflows and unlock the full potential of their data."

Diagnosis: 5 banned words, 2 banned phrases, weak verbs throughout.

Rewrite step by step:
- What is the writer trying to say? Our software helps users with complex workflows and helps them use their data better.
- Simplest version: "Our software helps users handle complex workflows and use their data."
- Add specificity: "Our software cuts the average workflow from 47 steps to 12, and turns your customer database into reports your team can actually read."
- Read aloud: works. Ship.

## What to do when the rule kills the rhythm

Sometimes following the rules produces a sentence that is correct but flat. Two strategies.

### Strategy 1: rebuild the rhythm with sentence-length variation

Vary your sentence lengths. A long sentence followed by a short one. A short sentence followed by a medium one. This is how human writers create music. AI defaults to medium-medium-medium.

Bad: "We help businesses grow. We use proven methods. We have many clients."
Better: "We help businesses grow, using methods that have worked for decades on every category we have touched. We have many clients. Most of them came back."

### Strategy 2: bring back a small specific image

Specifics carry rhythm even when the sentence is plain.

Bad: "Our coffee is fresh."
Better: "Our coffee was on a tree in Ethiopia three weeks ago."

The second version has the same propositional content (fresh coffee) but the image carries the line. No banned words. No fancy structure. Specific image does all the work.

### Strategy 3: stop and re-read the writer's own voice

If the rewrite has gone flat, you may have over-edited. Look at the original. What made it sound like the writer? Find the line that was most "them" and bring its energy back into the rewrite.

This is the difference between a copy editor and an editor. A copy editor enforces rules. An editor enforces rules and preserves voice. Be the editor.
