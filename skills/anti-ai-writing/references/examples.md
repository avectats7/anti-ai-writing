# Examples: 30 Before-and-After Pairs

Each example is a draft that came in sounding like an LLM and a rewrite that does not. The diagnosis line explains what was wrong. Use as a calibration set when applying the skill to a new draft.

Organised by format. The same patterns recur across formats; the cures are similar.

## Tweets and short social

### 1. Generic productivity hook

Before:
"In today's fast-paced world, leveraging cutting-edge AI tools to streamline your workflow is more crucial than ever. Unlock the potential of your productivity."

Diagnosis: 5 banned words (leveraging, cutting-edge, streamline, crucial, unlock), 2 banned phrases ("in today's fast-paced world," "unlock the potential of"), no concrete claim.

After:
"AI tools cut my Tuesday from 8 hours to 4. Same output. The trick was not adopting one tool. It was deleting three."

Why it works: trades the abstract for a specific number. Has a turn. Earns the reader's attention.

### 2. Founder announcement

Before:
"Today I'm thrilled to announce that we've launched our innovative new platform that empowers users to seamlessly navigate the complexities of modern finance."

Diagnosis: 4 banned words (innovative, empowers, seamlessly, navigate), "complexities of modern" is buzzword filler, "thrilled to announce" is the announcement template.

After:
"We launched. The product is a single page that shows what you owe across 4 banks, 7 cards, and 11 subscriptions, updated every morning. Try it free for the first month."

Why it works: shows the product instead of describing it. Specific counts. A real offer.

## Email subject lines and openers

### 3. Sales email subject

Before:
"Unlock the potential of your team's productivity with our innovative solution"

Diagnosis: classic LLM subject. Three banned words, one banned phrase, no specifics, no curiosity.

After:
"Cut your Tuesday standups from 30 minutes to 8"

Why it works: concrete promise, concrete number, single benefit.

### 4. Cold email opener

Before:
"I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to reach out because we've developed a comprehensive solution that could potentially revolutionize how your team operates."

Diagnosis: "hope this finds you well" is a tell, "comprehensive solution" is filler, "potentially revolutionize" hedges its own promise into mush.

After:
"I noticed your team posted three engineering roles in October. Hiring is the slowest part of growth. We make the screening loop one-fourth the length without losing signal. Worth a 15-minute call?"

Why it works: shows research, names the pain, makes a specific claim, asks for a small commitment.

### 5. Internal team email

Before:
"Furthermore, it is worth noting that the upcoming launch will play a pivotal role in our strategic objectives for the quarter."

Diagnosis: formulaic transition, hedging phrase, banned word "pivotal," "strategic objectives" is jargon.

After:
"The launch on the 18th is the biggest thing we ship this quarter. Three teams need to be in sync. Here is the timeline."

Why it works: names the date, names the stake, names the next thing.

## LinkedIn-style posts

### 6. Career reflection

Before:
"I'm thrilled to share that I've embarked on a transformative journey to unlock my full potential and capitalize on new opportunities in this ever-evolving landscape."

Diagnosis: 6 banned words (thrilled, embarked, transformative, unlock, capitalize, ever-evolving), one banned phrase ("full potential"), one banned phrase ("ever-evolving landscape"). Says nothing.

After:
"I quit my job last week. Five years was the right amount of time. Three things I learned. Two of them I would have skipped if I could. Open to talking with founders about the early hires they regret."

Why it works: a specific fact, a specific time, a specific offer to talk. The reader knows what the post is about.

### 7. Lessons-learned post

Before:
"Here are 5 key insights I've gathered from my journey that can empower you to navigate the complexities of leadership and unlock your team's full potential."

Diagnosis: rule of five list incoming, three banned words, "navigate the complexities" is buzzword filler.

After:
"Two things I got wrong as a first-time manager. One I have not forgiven myself for."

Why it works: changes the count (two, not five). Specific. The cliffhanger makes the reader keep reading.

### 8. Industry observation

Before:
"It's no secret that AI is revolutionizing the marketing landscape. Now more than ever, businesses must leverage these innovative tools to remain competitive."

Diagnosis: "it's no secret" is a hedger, four banned words, two banned phrases.

After:
"Most marketing teams are now using AI for first drafts. The ones who use it best are not the ones who write the cleverest prompts. They are the ones who edit hardest."

Why it works: takes a stance. Replaces the AI cliche with a specific observation about its use.

## Blog and Substack intros

### 9. Generic SaaS blog intro

Before:
"In today's fast-paced digital landscape, businesses are constantly seeking innovative ways to streamline their operations and unlock new opportunities for growth. This comprehensive guide will delve into the key strategies that can transform your approach."

Diagnosis: every word a tell. Six banned words, two banned phrases. Reader is asleep by sentence two.

After:
"Most business software is bought by IT and used by everyone else. The mismatch shows up six months in: the team has built workarounds in Slack and Google Docs that the software was supposed to prevent. This post is about what to do when you find yourself running the workaround layer."

Why it works: opens at a specific tension. Pictures the reader's situation. Sets up a payoff the post will deliver.

### 10. Personal essay opener

Before:
"As a passionate advocate for personal growth, I've come to realize that life is a journey filled with countless opportunities to delve deeper into our true potential."

Diagnosis: "passionate advocate" is filler, "journey" is a tell, "delve deeper" is a tell, "true potential" is a tell.

After:
"I used to think personal growth meant adding habits. Then I tried subtracting them. Subtraction was harder. And worked better."

Why it works: lead with a turn. The contrast does the work without needing to call itself a contrast.

## Marketing copy (headlines, hero sections, ad bodies)

### 11. SaaS hero headline

Before:
"Unlock the power of AI to streamline your business workflows and drive meaningful impact"

Diagnosis: 4 banned words/phrases. Plural buzzword soup.

After:
"AI for the four hours of weekly reporting you would rather not do"

Why it works: names the time saved. Names what the AI does. Hero copy that actually positions.

### 12. Landing page subhead

Before:
"Our comprehensive platform leverages cutting-edge technology to facilitate seamless collaboration across teams."

Diagnosis: 5 banned words. Says: we have software. Says nothing else.

After:
"One shared file. Every decision lives in it. Your team stops asking 'where is the latest version?'"

Why it works: concrete. Names the pain it removes. The headline + subhead now form an argument.

### 13. Pricing page benefits

Before:
"**Robust Features:** Our comprehensive suite empowers your team to maximize productivity.
**Seamless Integration:** Streamlined workflows that unlock the full potential of your tools.
**Innovative Solutions:** Cutting-edge technology designed to deliver meaningful impact."

Diagnosis: classic bullet-with-bold-title format. Every word a tell. Three interchangeable bullets.

After:
"Connects to the tools you already use. Email, calendar, Slack, your CRM. Setup takes 12 minutes. After that, your team types less and decides more."

Why it works: prose instead of decorated bullets. Specific tools named. Specific time named. A clear before/after for the customer.

### 14. Ad headline for a kitchen knife brand

Before:
"Unleash culinary excellence with our innovative, ergonomically designed knife"

Diagnosis: 2 banned words ("unleash," "innovative"), "culinary excellence" is filler.

After:
"The last kitchen knife you will need to sharpen yourself."

Why it works: one promise, specific. The reader pictures the future.

## About pages and bios

### 15. Solopreneur bio

Before:
"A passionate, results-driven professional with a proven track record of leveraging innovative strategies to deliver transformative outcomes in dynamic environments."

Diagnosis: every adjective a tell. Says nothing.

After:
"I help SaaS founders write the page that their cold traffic actually reads. Last year my pages added $4.1M in trial signups across nine clients. Based in Lisbon. Available six hours a week."

Why it works: names the work, names the proof, names the availability. The reader knows whether to email.

### 16. Company About page opener

Before:
"Founded in 2019, our innovative startup is on a mission to revolutionize the way teams collaborate and unlock their true potential through cutting-edge technology."

Diagnosis: 4 banned words, "true potential" is filler.

After:
"We started in 2019 because three of us were running the same Tuesday meeting at three different companies and none of us liked the meeting. The product is what we wished the meeting was."

Why it works: a real origin. A real grievance. A real connection from grievance to product.

## Internal communication

### 17. Project kickoff email

Before:
"Hi team, As we embark on this exciting new initiative, it's crucial that we align on our key objectives and leverage our collective expertise to drive meaningful outcomes."

Diagnosis: kitchen sink of AI tells. 5 banned words/phrases. Tells the team nothing.

After:
"Hi team. Project kickoff is Thursday. Here are the three decisions we need by then. Anna will run the meeting. Bring your last sprint's notes."

Why it works: names the date, names the decisions, names the runner, names what to bring. Email is now useful.

### 18. Quarterly update intro

Before:
"This past quarter has been a testament to our team's unwavering commitment to excellence. We've made significant strides in our journey to deliver transformative value to our customers."

Diagnosis: "testament to," "unwavering commitment," "significant strides," "journey," "transformative value." Five separate tells in two sentences.

After:
"We shipped four things this quarter. Two of them mattered. Here is what happened and what to expect next."

Why it works: honest count. Honest assessment. Reader keeps reading because they want to know which two.

## Spanish examples

### 19. LinkedIn post in Spanish

Before:
"Me complace compartir que he tenido el privilegio de aprovechar mi experiencia para potenciar el crecimiento de equipos diversos y fomentar una cultura de innovación en un panorama empresarial cada vez más complejo."

Diagnosis: "aprovechar," "potenciar," "fomentar," "panorama," "cada vez más" all banned in Spanish AI register. Plus "me complace compartir" is the Spanish equivalent of "I'm thrilled to share."

After:
"Renuncié la semana pasada. Cinco años fueron suficientes. Tres cosas que aprendí. Dos las habría evitado si pudiera. Si eres fundador y quieres hablar de tus primeras contrataciones, te leo."

Why it works: same fix as the English LinkedIn example. Concrete fact, concrete number, concrete offer.

### 20. Spanish marketing headline

Before:
"Potencia el potencial de tu negocio con soluciones innovadoras y robustas."

Diagnosis: "potencia," "potencial," "innovadoras," "robustas" all banned.

After:
"Tu negocio facturó X. Con nosotros, lo mismo. Pero los lunes vuelves a tener mañana libre."

Why it works: leaves the promise concrete (same revenue, less time). Uses the language a human would.

### 21. Spanish blog intro

Before:
"En el mundo actual, las empresas deben aprovechar las nuevas tecnologías para optimizar sus procesos y mantenerse competitivas en un mercado cada vez más dinámico."

Diagnosis: "en el mundo actual," "aprovechar," "optimizar," "cada vez más," "dinámico." Five tells.

After:
"La empresa promedio paga ocho herramientas que hacen lo mismo. Este post es sobre cuál vale la pena y cuál estás pagando por costumbre."

Why it works: starts with a specific observation. Promises a specific value. No buzzword filler.

## Edge cases worth studying

### 22. When a "banned" word is legitimately technical

Before (a developer blog post):
"We chose Rails as our framework because it allows us to leverage convention over configuration."

Diagnosis: "framework" is a legitimate technical term here (Rails is a named software framework). "Leverage" is filler.

After:
"We chose Rails because its convention-over-configuration approach saved us months of setup."

Why it works: kept the technical term that was carrying real meaning. Removed the filler verb. Replaced with concrete benefit.

### 23. When the writer's voice is intentionally formal

Before (a legal brief intro, where formality is the norm):
"The plaintiff respectfully submits that, in light of the foregoing, the court should additionally consider the impact on subsequent proceedings, which is of paramount importance."

Diagnosis: "additionally," "subsequent," "paramount" are all banned in normal prose. In a legal brief, formality is part of the genre.

After (light edit, keeping formal voice):
"The plaintiff respectfully submits that the court should consider the impact on later proceedings, which is critical."

Why it works: kept the legal register. Replaced "additionally" with a clean construction, "subsequent" with "later," "paramount" with "critical." Voice preserved, tells removed.

### 24. When quoted material contains AI tells

Before (a critique article):
'Sam Altman recently said, "We are about to unlock the true potential of artificial intelligence." This is the kind of claim that...'

Diagnosis: the quote contains banned words ("unlock," "true potential"). But the quote must be preserved verbatim.

After (no change to the quote, light edit to the surrounding prose):
'Sam Altman recently said, "We are about to unlock the true potential of artificial intelligence." His phrasing is itself the giveaway. The "true potential" framing has become the boilerplate of AI executives speaking about AI...'

Why it works: keeps the quote accurate. Turns the critique INTO a comment on the AI default register. The banned words now do critical work, not stylistic damage.
